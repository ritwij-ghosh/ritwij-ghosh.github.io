Qualification of In-Situ Melt Pool Monitoring in Laser Powder Bed Fusion

Research Context

This work was conducted as a Research Intern at Georgia Tech's AI in Manufacturing Pilot Facility (AIMFP), where I focused on applying computer vision techniques to additive manufacturing quality control. Laser powder bed fusion (LPBF) is a 3D printing process that builds metal parts layer-by-layer using a high-power laser. Defects introduced during printing — such as porosity, lack of fusion, or keyhole collapse — are difficult to detect after the fact, making in-situ (real-time) monitoring critical.

CV Pipeline

I trained U-Net and CNN-based segmentation models on high-resolution melt pool images captured during the LPBF process. Each image captures the thermal signature of the melt pool — the region where the laser is fusing metal powder. Anomalies in the melt pool shape, size, or intensity distribution are early indicators of layer-wise defects.

The pipeline includes aggressive data augmentation (random crops, brightness/contrast jitter, horizontal flips) to handle the limited dataset size typical of manufacturing research. Custom loss functions combining Dice loss with binary cross-entropy improved segmentation accuracy on small, high-contrast defect regions. Models were evaluated using IoU and F1 metrics per defect class.

3D Visualization & Expert Review Tool

Beyond model training, I built a Napari-based 3D CAD viewer that allows engineers to inspect defect predictions layer-by-layer in three dimensions. By stacking per-layer segmentation masks and rendering them as a volumetric defect map, the tool makes it possible to identify defect clusters and trace their spatial distribution through the printed part. This cut expert review time by 65% compared to manually inspecting individual frame sequences.

Publication

This research culminated in a co-authored paper: "Qualification of In-Situ Melt Pool Monitoring in Laser Powder Bed Fusion."
